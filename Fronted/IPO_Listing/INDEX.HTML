Whether to buy an IPO on the first day depends on your investment strategy and risk tolerance. IPOs can be volatile on the first day of trading. If you believe in the company's long-term prospects and have done thorough research, investing on the first day might be suitable. However, waiting to see how the market reacts can provide more information for making an informed decision.

To evaluate if an IPO is good, consider these factors:

  • • Company's financial health and growth trajectory
  • • Management team's experience and track record
  • • Business model and competitive advantages
  • • Market potential and industry trends
  • • Valuation relative to peers
  • • Use of IPO proceeds
  • • Risk factors mentioned in the prospectus

You can check IPO start dates through:

  • • Stock exchange websites (NSE, BSE)
  • • SEBI website
  • • Financial news portals
  • • Your brokerage platform
  • • Company announcements and Red Herring Prospectus

Issue size refers to the total amount of money a company aims to raise through its IPO. It is calculated by multiplying the number of shares offered in the IPO by the issue price per share. The issue size indicates the scale of the offering and can influence investor interest and market liquidity after listing.

The number of shares in a lot varies for each IPO and is determined by the company. SEBI regulations require that the minimum application value should be within the range of ₹10,000 to ₹15,000. Based on the issue price, the lot size is calculated to fall within this range. For example, if the issue price is ₹500, the lot size might be set at 30 shares (₹15,000 total value).

Lot size is calculated based on SEBI's guidelines for minimum application value:

  • • SEBI mandates a minimum application value between ₹10,000 and ₹15,000
  • • Lot size = Minimum application value ÷ Issue price (rounded to nearest whole number)
  • • For example: If issue price is ₹350, and minimum application value is set at ₹14,000, then lot size = ₹14,000 ÷ ₹350 = 40 shares

The IPO price band is decided by the company in consultation with the Book Running Lead Managers (BRLMs) or investment bankers. They consider factors such as:

  • • Company's financial performance and growth prospects
  • • Valuation of comparable companies in the same sector
  • • Current market conditions and investor sentiment
  • • Feedback from institutional investors during pre-IPO roadshows
  • • Overall demand expectations

IPO GMP (Grey Market Premium) is the premium amount at which IPO shares are traded in the unofficial/grey market before they are officially listed on the stock exchange. It indicates the market sentiment and potential listing gains:

  • • Positive GMP suggests the stock might list above its issue price
  • • Negative GMP suggests the stock might list below its issue price
  • • GMP is unofficial and can fluctuate significantly based on market sentiment
  • • It should not be the sole factor for investment decisions

The number of lots you should apply for an IPO depends on:

  • • Your investment budget and risk appetite
  • • Expected demand for the IPO (higher demand means lower allotment chances)
  • • Retail investors can apply for up to 13-14 lots (maximum ₹2 lakhs) in the retail category
  • • Applying for multiple lots increases investment amount but doesn't necessarily improve allotment chances
  • • For heavily oversubscribed IPOs, even single-lot applications may face a lottery-based allotment
